# Cartwheel & Co. — SQL Analytics Showcase

A self-contained SQL analytics project: a realistic e-commerce SQLite database (five related tables, ~11,000 rows) plus **15 analytical queries** that progress from basic aggregations to window functions, RFM segmentation, and cohort retention analysis. Every query is annotated with the business question it answers and an interpretation of the actual result.

The dataset is generated by a seeded script, so every number quoted below reproduces exactly on your machine.

## The dataset

Cartwheel & Co. is a fictional online retailer selling across six categories (Electronics, Home & Kitchen, Sports & Outdoors, Beauty, Toys & Games, Office Supplies) from early 2023 through mid-2025.

```
categories 1──* products 1──* order_items *──1 orders *──1 customers
```

| Table | Rows | Contents |
|---|---|---|
| `customers` | 1,500 | signup date, city/state, acquisition channel |
| `orders` | 3,523 | order date and status (completed / returned / cancelled) |
| `order_items` | 6,119 | line items with the price actually paid |
| `products` | 150 | catalog with list price and unit cost |
| `categories` | 6 | product taxonomy |

The generator ([generate_data.py](generate_data.py), standard library only) builds in realistic behavior rather than uniform noise: the customer base grows over time, November–December orders spike, product popularity is long-tailed, ~20% of line items are promo-priced, and repeat-purchase propensity differs by acquisition channel. That's what gives the queries something worth finding.

## Three findings worth your attention

**1. Referral customers are worth ~54% more than paid-search customers.**
Referral buyers average **3.46 orders and $682 of lifetime revenue** each, versus $442 for paid search and $508 for organic (Q7). They're also 6 of the top 10 customers by lifetime spend despite being only 15% of signups (Q5). If one channel gets the next marketing dollar, it's the referral program.

**2. The 2025 revenue slide is an acquisition problem, not a churn problem.**
Revenue peaked at $64k in November 2024 and fell steadily through 2025 (Q2, Q10). Splitting each month's revenue into new vs. returning customers (Q12) shows why: returning-customer revenue held firm while new-customer revenue collapsed from ~$20k to under $8k a month, pushing the returning share above 74%. Loyalty is fine — the top of the funnel is what dried up.

**3. Revenue is concentrated enough to be a risk.**
The top 10% of customers contribute **32.7%** of revenue and the top 30% contribute two-thirds (Q14). On the catalog side, just two products — the Portable Monitor and the Smart Mouse — account for a quarter of all revenue, and Electronics as a category takes 57.7% (Q3, Q4). A stock-out on one SKU or the loss of a few dozen top customers would be immediately visible in the P&L.

## The queries

[queries.sql](queries.sql) is organized in four sections of increasing difficulty:

| Section | Queries | Techniques |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Foundations | Q1–Q4 | `GROUP BY` aggregation, filtering, subquery for share-of-total |
| 2. Multi-table joins | Q5–Q7 | 3–4 table joins, `LEFT JOIN` for absence ("never ordered"), per-group rates |
| 3. Window functions | Q8–Q11 | running totals (`SUM OVER`), `RANK ... PARTITION BY`, `LAG` for MoM growth and inter-order gaps |
| 4. Advanced | Q12–Q15 | multi-CTE pipelines, `NTILE` (RFM segmentation, Pareto deciles), cohort retention matrix |

Each query carries a comment block with the business question and a short interpretation of its output, so the file reads as an analysis, not a syntax demo.

## How to run

Requires Python 3.8+ (no packages to install) and any SQLite client.

```bash
git clone <this-repo>
cd retail-sql-analytics

# 1. build the database (~1 second, deterministic)
python generate_data.py

# 2. run the queries
sqlite3 retail.db ".read queries.sql"
```

No `sqlite3` CLI? On Python 3.12+ the stdlib ships one — `python -m sqlite3 retail.db` — or open `retail.db` in [DB Browser for SQLite](https://sqlitebrowser.org/) and paste queries from `queries.sql`.
